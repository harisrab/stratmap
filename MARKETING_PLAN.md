# Stratbook Marketing Plan

Last updated: 2026-05-10

## Executive Summary

Stratbook should be marketed as a new category, not as a generic AI app: **a map-first AI research workspace for people who think in places, evidence, and briefings**.

The cheapest path to traction is not paid ads. It is to get Stratbook into the digital highways where OSINT researchers, geopolitical analysts, journalists, GIS/GEOINT people, think-tank researchers, defense-adjacent planners, and map-curious knowledge workers already travel:

- OSINT newsletters, tool roundups, and Discords.
- Reddit communities and practitioner forums.
- Geospatial/GIS publications and newsletters.
- Product launch communities for technical early adopters.
- X/LinkedIn creators who teach OSINT, geolocation, satellite imagery, and intelligence workflows.
- Public example stratbooks that can be shared as useful artifacts, not as ads.

The core marketing loop should be:

1. Publish a useful public stratbook or guide.
2. Share it in one relevant community with a non-promotional, methodology-first post.
3. Ask for workflow feedback.
4. Turn feedback into product fixes, article improvements, and new examples.
5. Re-share the improved artifact through newsletters, creators, and launch surfaces.

The goal for the first 90 days is not vanity traffic. It is to find the smallest group of users who say: “I already do this work, and this map-first workflow is obviously better than my current mess.”

## Positioning

### One-Line Position

Stratbook is a map-first AI research workspace for turning notes, sources, and geographic context into strategic briefings.

### Category Frame

Stratbook sits between:

- GIS tools, which are powerful but often heavy for day-to-day research.
- Markdown/knowledge-base tools, which are good for notes but weak on geography.
- AI research tools, which can summarize but often lose source and location context.
- Story map tools, which are strong for publishing but less suited to messy research-in-progress.

### Core Audiences

1. **OSINT researchers and geolocation learners**
   - Pain: sources, screenshots, coordinates, and confidence notes are scattered.
   - Offer: pin evidence to locations and turn it into a spatial brief.

2. **Geopolitical analysts and think-tank researchers**
   - Pain: regional research lives across documents, maps, timelines, and source folders.
   - Offer: build a living map of the issue and brief from it.

3. **Foreign-desk reporters and investigative journalists**
   - Pain: verification work is hard to explain and hard to preserve.
   - Offer: publish a map-backed research artifact with sources and context.

4. **Geo-aware investors and risk teams**
   - Pain: infrastructure, ports, conflict zones, supply chains, and facilities need spatial monitoring.
   - Offer: maintain watchlists and briefings by place.

5. **GIS/GEOINT-adjacent professionals**
   - Pain: GIS handles data, but not always analyst narrative, notes, and briefing workflow.
   - Offer: a lighter research and briefing layer upstream of formal GIS.

## North Star

Make Stratbook the product people mention when someone asks:

- “What tool should I use to organize OSINT research on a map?”
- “How do I turn geolocation notes into a brief?”
- “Is there a Notion/Obsidian-like workspace for spatial research?”
- “What is a lightweight alternative to StoryMaps for analysis-in-progress?”
- “How do I publish map-backed research without building a custom GIS site?”

## Digital Highways

### OSINT Highways

These are the first places to learn, contribute, and earn trust.

- [Sector035 / Week in OSINT](https://sector035.nl/)
  - Why it matters: high-signal OSINT curation and tool discovery.
  - Approach: submit a genuinely useful guide or public stratbook, not a sales pitch.
  - Best asset: “What Is an OSINT Map Tool?” plus an example stratbook.

- [Bellingcat Online Investigation Toolkit](https://bellingcat.gitbook.io/toolkit)
  - Why it matters: canonical OSINT tool taxonomy and practitioner reference.
  - Approach: build useful OSINT workflows that could eventually be considered for tool lists or community mentions.
  - Best asset: a public OSINT map template with methodology notes.

- [r/OSINT](https://www.reddit.com/r/OSINT/)
  - Why it matters: large practitioner and learner community.
  - Approach: post methodology, examples, and templates. Avoid “please try my app.”
  - Best post format: “I made a map-based template for organizing OSINT geolocation notes. What fields are missing?”

- [Project Owl OSINT Discord](https://discord.com/servers/project-owl-the-osint-community-518979695702704132)
  - Why it matters: real-time OSINT community and event-monitoring overlap.
  - Approach: join, observe norms, share a practical workflow only after participating.

- [The Weekly OSINT Newsletter](https://www.osintnewsletter.osint-jobs.com/)
  - Why it matters: OSINT audience already consumes tools and methods through newsletters.
  - Approach: pitch a useful resource: “map note template for geolocation work.”

- [OSINTBench newsletters guide](https://osintbench.com/guides/osint-newsletters/)
  - Why it matters: directory of newsletter targets and OSINT information sources.
  - Approach: use as a target list for outreach and creator mapping.

### Geospatial / GIS / GEOINT Highways

- [Directions Magazine](https://www.directionsmag.com/newsletter/subscription)
  - Why it matters: geospatial professionals and location-intelligence readers.
  - Approach: pitch a thought piece on “lightweight GEOINT workflows outside enterprise GIS.”

- [Geospatial World / GeoIntelligence Magazine](https://geospatialworld.net/magazines/geointelligence-magazine/)
  - Why it matters: geospatial and GEOINT audience with global reach.
  - Approach: submit a practical article or request inclusion for a public example.

- [The Esri Brief](https://www.esri.com/en-us/news-publications/esri-brief)
  - Why it matters: mainstream location-intelligence framing.
  - Approach: monitor language and themes; direct inclusion may be hard, but it informs positioning.

- [CARTO Spatial Dispatch](https://go.carto.com/urban-geo-newsletter)
  - Why it matters: modern cloud-native geospatial analytics audience.
  - Approach: learn what data/analytics readers care about; pitch only once Stratbook has a strong example.

- GIS and geospatial Reddit communities
  - Examples: `r/gis`, `r/geospatial`, `r/MapPorn` only for visual artifacts, not product posts.
  - Approach: ask for feedback on map-first research workflows and example artifacts.

### Product Discovery Highways

Use these after the first real examples and testimonials exist.

- [Product Hunt](https://www.producthunt.com/)
  - Best timing: after 5-10 real users and 2-3 polished public stratbooks.
  - Launch angle: “AI research workspace where every note starts on a map.”

- [Hacker News Show HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/show)
  - Best timing: when the demo is fast, public, and technically interesting.
  - Launch angle: “Show HN: I built a map-first research workspace for OSINT/geopolitical analysis.”

- [Indie Hackers](https://www.indiehackers.com/)
  - Best use: founder journey, feedback, bootstrapped launch story.
  - Launch angle: building in public around a real personal need.

- [BetaList](https://betalist.com/)
  - Best use: collect early beta signups once the onboarding flow is crisp.

- [LaunchBoard](https://www.launchboard.dev/)
  - Best use: low-cost discovery/backlink surface for indie products.

## Guerrilla Marketing Plays

### 1. Public Stratbook Drops

Publish narrow, useful public examples:

- Red Sea shipping chokepoints.
- Taiwan Strait flashpoint atlas.
- Russia-Ukraine operational map.
- Port/infrastructure watchlist.
- Border-area event chronology.
- Energy infrastructure risk map.

Each public stratbook should include:

- A strong title.
- Date updated.
- Short methodology note.
- Source list.
- Map pins with confidence labels.
- A short briefing summary.
- A “fork this stratbook” or “use this template” action.

Distribution:

- Post the artifact in one relevant community.
- Ask for methodological feedback.
- Update based on comments.
- Then pitch the improved version to newsletters.

### 2. Free Templates

Create free templates that are useful even before someone becomes a paying user:

- OSINT geolocation note template.
- GEOINT monitoring template.
- Intelligence brief template.
- Conflict chronology template.
- Port watchlist template.
- Source confidence rubric.

Distribution:

- Blog/resources page.
- Reddit posts.
- Newsletter outreach.
- LinkedIn/X threads.
- “Download markdown template” pages for SEO.

### 3. Methodology Threads

Publish short threads/posts:

- “How to structure an OSINT map note.”
- “A 7-field template for geolocation confidence.”
- “How to turn map pins into a briefing.”
- “Why analysts should separate facts, assumptions, and hypotheses on maps.”
- “How to maintain an area-of-interest watchlist.”

CTA:

- “I turned this into a public Stratbook/template here.”

### 4. Community Feedback Loops

Every community post should ask for specific feedback:

- “What fields are missing from this OSINT note template?”
- “Would this help you teach geolocation?”
- “What would make this credible enough for a newsroom?”
- “Where does this break for real analysts?”

This reduces spam perception and increases useful replies.

### 5. Founder-Led Build in Public

Post regularly from the founder account:

- Why the product exists.
- Screenshots of real workflows.
- Before/after examples.
- Lessons from users.
- Research snippets.
- Public stratbook drops.

Best channels:

- X for OSINT/geopolitical creator discovery.
- LinkedIn for geospatial, risk, journalism, and think-tank credibility.
- Hacker News/Indie Hackers for technical founder/product discovery.

## Launch Sequence

### Phase 1: Private Feedback, No Hype

Duration: 2 weeks.

Goal:

- Validate the workflow with practitioners.
- Collect language users naturally use.
- Identify missing fields/features.

Actions:

- 15 individual outreach messages.
- 5 workflow calls.
- 3 public examples.
- 3 template pages.
- Fix onboarding friction.

Success criteria:

- 5 people say they would use it for a real project.
- 3 people ask for access or share it with someone.
- Clear pattern in use-case language.

### Phase 2: Community Seeding

Duration: 3-4 weeks.

Goal:

- Get feedback and organic mentions from niche communities.

Actions:

- Reddit methodology posts.
- OSINT Discord participation.
- LinkedIn posts for geospatial/workflow angle.
- X threads for OSINT/geolocation angle.
- Newsletter submissions.

Success criteria:

- 500+ qualified visitors.
- 50+ signups.
- 10+ active projects.
- 2-3 newsletter/community mentions.

### Phase 3: Public Launch

Duration: 1 week launch window.

Goal:

- Turn validated niche interest into broader technical/product discovery.

Actions:

- Product Hunt launch.
- Show HN launch.
- Indie Hackers post.
- Founder launch thread.
- Email all early users.
- Ask existing users to comment with real use cases.

Success criteria:

- 2,000+ visitors.
- 200+ signups.
- 25+ active projects.
- 5+ meaningful inbound conversations.

### Phase 4: Paid Experiments

Only start paid after organic signals show which audience converts.

Small paid tests:

- Sponsor a niche OSINT newsletter.
- Sponsor a geospatial newsletter.
- Retarget site visitors.
- Promote one high-performing template.
- Sponsor an OSINT/geolocation challenge.

Do not start with broad Google or X ads. The audience is too specific and the positioning still needs learning.

## Metrics

### Leading Indicators

- Replies from practitioners.
- Public stratbook forks.
- Template downloads.
- Time on article pages.
- Users creating second project.
- Users adding multiple pins/notes.
- Users sharing a public stratbook.

### Acquisition Metrics

- Visitors by channel.
- Signup conversion rate.
- Activation rate: created first stratbook.
- Useful activation rate: created 5+ pins or notes.
- Share rate: public link created.
- Referral source by signed-up user.

### Qualitative Metrics

Track exact phrases users say:

- “I currently use...”
- “I wish this could...”
- “I would use this for...”
- “This replaces...”
- “This is not useful until...”

These phrases should feed SEO pages, homepage copy, onboarding, and outreach.

## Rules of Engagement

- Never spam communities.
- Lead with useful artifacts.
- Ask for critique before asking for promotion.
- Be clear about safety and ethics in OSINT contexts.
- Avoid encouraging targeting private individuals.
- Make every public example narrow, source-linked, and method-backed.
- Do not buy ads until one organic channel shows conversion.
