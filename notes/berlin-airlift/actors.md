## Western Allies

The U.S. and Britain built a logistics machine whose credibility depended on repetitive, disciplined air throughput rather than dramatic battlefield actions.


### Airlift system




#### U.S. Airlift Task Force

American C-47 and C-54 operations formed the backbone of early and later high-volume airlift into Berlin.

- Role: Heavy-lift air bridge
- Geographic anchor: Centered on Tempelhof and corridor traffic into the U.S. sector of the city.
- Why it matters: The U.S. contribution turned the blockade into a throughput competition rather than a surrender timetable.
- What to monitor: Aircraft availability, load discipline, weather, and turnaround tempo.


#### Royal Air Force Berlin Airlift

British airlift activity concentrated on sustaining its sectors and sharing the burden of the corridor system.

- Role: Sector sustainment and corridor traffic
- Geographic anchor: Operated strongly through RAF Gatow and the British sector, while sharing the wider corridor rhythm of the lift.
- Why it matters: The airlift only worked because multiple national systems accepted common discipline and timing.
- What to monitor: Weather resilience, interoperability, and night or winter operations.


#### West Berlin municipal administration

City authorities were the demand manager of the crisis, translating aircraft arrivals into electricity, heat, and food for residents.

- Role: Urban demand and distribution management
- Geographic anchor: Centered on the city sectors and the urban network receiving supplies from the airfields.
- Why it matters: The airlift's success depended on whether supplies reached citizens quickly enough to prevent political collapse.
- What to monitor: Coal distribution, civilian morale, and winter vulnerability.




## Soviet blockade authorities

The Soviet Union attempted to pressure the Western sectors by cutting land and water access while avoiding direct air conflict inside the established corridors.


### Blockade pressure




#### Soviet blockade authorities

The blockade combined political pressure with physical denial of road, rail, and barge access into West Berlin.

- Role: Access denial and coercive pressure
- Geographic anchor: Controlled the land and water approaches around the city while leaving the formal air corridors open.
- Why it matters: The blockade defined the crisis by forcing the Western response into the air.
- What to monitor: Duration of pressure and whether logistical exhaustion could produce political concessions.
