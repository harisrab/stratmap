## Geography and map logic

Berlin was an island of Western-controlled sectors deep inside Soviet-controlled territory. The city could only be sustained through a narrow set of agreed air corridors and a handful of airfields capable of absorbing intense traffic.


### Corridors over the center of Germany

The airlift depended on fixed corridors that gave traffic predictable legal and navigational paths, but also limited flexibility if weather or congestion disrupted the schedule.


### Tempelhof, Gatow, and Tegel as throughput nodes

The airlift was won or lost at the airfields. Handling, turnaround time, runway quality, and unloading efficiency mattered as much as the number of aircraft assigned.


### Urban demand as the invisible geography

Coal, food, and essentials had to flow through the same limited air system. The city itself was the demand signal pulling every ton through the corridors.
