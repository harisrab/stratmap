## Timeline and turning points

This notebook carries scenario events so you can scrub the time bar, jump between beats,
and compare how actors and geography shift together.


### Currency reform sharpens the crisis
**20 June 1948**

Western moves on currency reform intensified Soviet concerns over the political future of Germany and accelerated the standoff that soon reached Berlin's transport arteries.


### The Soviet blockade begins
**24 June 1948**

Road, rail, and barge access to the Western sectors was interrupted, making the air corridors the only workable route for sustaining West Berlin.


### The sustained airlift begins
**26 June 1948**

The Western allies committed to continuous supply flights, transforming a temporary workaround into the central strategy for resisting the blockade.


### Tegel expands the airfield network
**Late 1948**

The rapid development of Tegel helped reduce congestion and gave the airlift more margin as winter demand and traffic intensity rose.


### Easter Parade demonstrates peak throughput
**16 April 1949**

Record tonnage on Easter Parade day proved that the airlift had matured into a disciplined, high-output logistics system capable of defeating the blockade politically.


### The blockade is lifted
**12 May 1949**

The Soviet Union reopened land access, but flights continued for months to build reserves and prove that the city would not again be easily coerced.
