## Soviet and Cuban missile complex

Soviet deployments in Cuba altered the strategic map, while Cuban air defense and state control shaped what the U.S. could observe and threaten.


### Missile and air defense sites




#### Soviet missile regiments in Cuba

The offensive missile deployment was the heart of the crisis and the reason every reconnaissance image mattered so much.

- Role: Strategic missile deployment
- Geographic anchor: Distributed across western and central Cuba, including the San Cristobal area and other launch complexes.
- Why it matters: The sites transformed Cuba from a political problem into a nuclear warning-time problem.
- What to monitor: Construction status, missile readiness, and whether launch complexes remained under Soviet operational control.


#### Cuban air defense network

Cuban and Soviet-controlled air defense systems shaped reconnaissance risk and the danger of accidental escalation.

- Role: Air defense and reconnaissance denial
- Geographic anchor: Spread across Cuba with particular relevance near sensitive missile areas and key western approach routes.
- Why it matters: Air defense mattered because the U.S. needed continuing intelligence, but each flight carried escalation risk.
- What to monitor: Rules of engagement, radar coverage, and the danger created by repeated overflights.




## United States response

American decision-making split across intelligence collection, naval quarantine operations, and broader strategic readiness.


### Policy and maritime enforcement




#### U.S. quarantine force

Naval power projected the U.S. response onto a visible line at sea, allowing coercion without immediate strikes on Cuba.

- Role: Naval quarantine and interception force
- Geographic anchor: Positioned north and east of Cuba to regulate the maritime approaches during the quarantine.
- Why it matters: The quarantine created a physical line where resolve could be shown while preserving space for diplomacy.
- What to monitor: Boarding decisions, Soviet ship movements, and whether naval pressure remained proportional.


#### ExComm and strategic readiness system

Civilian and military crisis managers had to interpret intelligence, signal resolve, and avoid a sequence of moves that closed off diplomacy.

- Role: Strategic command and crisis decision node
- Geographic anchor: Centered on Washington and the wider U.S. strategic posture, but operationally tied to reconnaissance and the maritime quarantine around Cuba.
- Why it matters: The crisis was governed as much from briefing rooms and command centers as from ships and launch complexes.
- What to monitor: Escalation control, intelligence confidence, and the handling of simultaneous military and diplomatic channels.
