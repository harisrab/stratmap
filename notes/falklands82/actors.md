## United Kingdom

British forces had to combine political signaling, sea control, amphibious lift, and a final ground advance over austere terrain.

### Task force and landing force

The British campaign depended on a carrier-centered fleet, the staging network behind it, and the brigade that would eventually come ashore.

#### Task Force 317

Carrier-led expeditionary force built to recover the islands and establish sea and air access long enough for an amphibious landing.

- Role: Long-range maritime strike and command force
- Geographic anchor: Moved from the North Atlantic staging chain through [Ascension Island](stratmap://map?lat=-7.9467&lon=-14.3559&zoom=8) into waters east of the Falklands.
- Why it matters: It linked politics to action; without it there was no credible way to isolate the islands or protect the landing force.
- What to monitor: Carrier aircraft availability, submarine effects, and vulnerability while massed around the landing area.

#### 3 Commando Brigade

The brigade that translated British sea access into a land campaign, first through the San Carlos landing and then by pushing east toward Stanley.

- Role: Amphibious and light infantry ground force
- Geographic anchor: Concentrated around [San Carlos Water](stratmap://map?lat=-51.566&lon=-59.08&zoom=11) and later moved along the East Falkland ridgelines toward the capital.
- Why it matters: Its movement from beachhead to high ground turned an amphibious lodgment into a campaign with a clear end state.
- What to monitor: How the beachhead, helicopter lift, and terrain combined to set the tempo of the final approach.

## Argentina

Argentina held the islands but had to defend a distant garrison under growing maritime isolation while sustaining air attack from the mainland.

### Island garrison and mainland air arm

#### Argentine Falklands Garrison

The island defense centered on Port Stanley, local positions, and the need to absorb British pressure from the beachhead inward.

- Role: Fixed defense and local maneuver force
- Geographic anchor: Anchored on [Port Stanley](stratmap://map?lat=-51.6938&lon=-57.8517&zoom=11) and the ridgelines west of the town, with limited freedom to reposition under British artillery and air pressure.
- Why it matters: Once the British landed, the garrison became a race between defensive cohesion and encirclement.
- What to monitor: Ground held, mobility available, and whether mainland support could still change the balance.

#### Rio Grande Strike Wing

Mainland air power gave Argentina its sharpest operational reach, but the distance to the islands narrowed loiter time and made every sortie count.

- Role: Strike aircraft staging and launch network
- Geographic anchor: Based around [Rio Grande](stratmap://map?lat=-53.7877&lon=-67.7095&zoom=10) in Tierra del Fuego and projected west-to-east toward shipping off East Falkland.
- Why it matters: Its attacks forced the British fleet to disperse, improvise air defense, and accept risk around the landing area.
- What to monitor: Sortie density, weapon effectiveness, and how distance compressed tactical options.
