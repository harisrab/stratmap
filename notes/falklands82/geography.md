## Geography and map logic

The Falklands campaign looks compact on a modern map, but the decisive problem was range. The British had to project force from the United Kingdom through Ascension Island and then into waters where weather, sea state, and Argentine air attack all compressed decision time.


### Ascension as the hinge

Ascension Island was not the battlefield, but it was the forward hinge that made the campaign possible. It mattered for refueling, staging, and for turning political intent into an actual fleet movement.


### San Carlos Water as a landing geometry problem

San Carlos was useful because its inlets and terrain provided some shelter from open-ocean attack. The same narrow water also concentrated ships, helicopters, and landing craft into a small battlespace.


### Stanley as the political center of gravity

Port Stanley was the administrative and symbolic heart of the campaign. Once British ground forces closed on Stanley, the geography of the rest of the islands mattered less than the final encirclement and the sustainability of the Argentine defense.
