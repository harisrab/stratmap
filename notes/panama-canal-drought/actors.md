## Canal and watershed system

The canal operator and watershed are inseparable in a drought year because water management becomes the core operational question.


### Canal operator and water system




#### Panama Canal Authority

The authority had to translate low water into draft limits, transit caps, booking rules, and public guidance for global shipping.

- Role: Water-constrained transit manager
- Geographic anchor: Centered on the canal corridor itself, with special operational attention on Gatun Lake and the lock system.
- Why it matters: It was the actor turning climate stress into operational limits that shippers could not ignore.
- What to monitor: Lake levels, transit caps, booking changes, and water-saving measures.


#### Gatun Lake watershed system

The watershed and lake levels determined how much water could be used for ship lockages and, by extension, how much transit capacity remained.

- Role: Hydrologic constraint
- Geographic anchor: Focused on Gatun Lake and the broader catchment feeding the canal.
- Why it matters: It is the environmental engine behind every operational restriction described in the notebook.
- What to monitor: Rainfall deficits, lake elevation, and how water-saving measures change daily operations.




## Shipping demand

Ships did not simply disappear when restrictions tightened; they queued, bid for slots, rerouted, or turned toward intermodal workarounds.


### Ships and cargo owners




#### Container carriers

Container lines needed schedule reliability, making them highly sensitive to queue length and slot uncertainty.

- Role: Schedule-sensitive canal demand
- Geographic anchor: Visible in Atlantic and Pacific queue areas and in the booking competition for restricted slots.
- Why it matters: Carrier behavior is the clearest operational signal that canal restrictions have crossed into a network disruption story.
- What to monitor: Queue tolerance, slot bidding, and intermodal substitution.


#### Energy and bulk shippers

Bulk and energy cargoes faced their own decisions about draft, slot availability, and whether alternate routes would be more predictable.

- Role: Draft-sensitive canal demand
- Geographic anchor: Concentrated in queue areas and in the wider route calculations shaped by limited daily transits.
- Why it matters: They make clear that the canal's drought is not just a container story but a broader commodity-routing problem.
- What to monitor: Draft limits, waiting cost, and alternative routes or terminals.
