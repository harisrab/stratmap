## Timeline and turning points

This notebook carries scenario events so you can scrub the time bar, jump between beats,
and compare how actors and geography shift together.


### Low-water concerns move from forecast to operations
**June 2023**

By early summer, reduced rainfall and reservoir stress had become an operational concern rather than a purely hydrologic warning.


### Draft restrictions tighten
**July 2023**

Restrictions on draft signaled that water scarcity was directly affecting the types of ships and loads the canal could efficiently handle.


### Transit caps draw global attention
**30 August 2023**

Limits on daily ship transits made the drought visible to global shipping markets and pushed carriers to reassess slot competition and queue tolerance.


### Queues and booking competition peak
**Late 2023**

By late 2023, slot scarcity and queue build-up had turned the canal into a scheduling and bidding story as much as an infrastructure one.


### Water-saving and booking adjustments continue
**Early 2024**

Operational changes continued into 2024 as the authority tried to match transit demand to a still-constrained water system.


### Improving rains begin to ease the tightest restrictions
**Mid-2024**

As water conditions improved, some pressure on the system eased, but the drought had already demonstrated how exposed global shipping is to the canal's freshwater balance.
