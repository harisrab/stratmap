## Launch and coercion network

The operational logic was to threaten shipping and impose uncertainty from Yemen's Red Sea littoral.


### Yemeni coastal pressure




#### Houthi coastal launch network

Missile, drone, and maritime seizure capability positioned to pressure ships transiting toward or through the strait.

- Role: Coastal interdiction and signaling network
- Geographic anchor: Anchored along the Yemeni Red Sea coast, especially around Hodeidah and approaches facing the southern Red Sea.
- Why it matters: It turned a regional conflict into a maritime insurance and route-planning problem for global trade.
- What to monitor: Launch tempo, target selection, and whether pressure remains focused on high-profile shipping or spreads more broadly.




## Security response

Naval patrols aimed to protect transit, intercept threats, and reassure commercial operators without fully eliminating risk.


### Escort and patrol missions




#### Operation Prosperity Guardian

U.S.-led maritime protection effort that concentrated on escort, air defense, and reassurance for commercial shipping.

- Role: Escort and interception mission
- Geographic anchor: Operated across the southern Red Sea and Gulf of Aden near the main shipping lane.
- Why it matters: Its presence helped keep some traffic moving and established a shared reference frame for risk reporting and response.
- What to monitor: How much escort presence is enough to restore confidence rather than merely react to attacks.


#### EUNAVFOR ASPIDES

European maritime mission focused on defensive escort and protection of freedom of navigation in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden.

- Role: European protection mission
- Geographic anchor: Built its picture around the same chokepoint system but with a distinct European command framework and ship set.
- Why it matters: It demonstrated how a trade shock can rapidly produce separate but overlapping security architectures.
- What to monitor: Patrol coverage, convoy timing, and the relationship between European, U.S., and regional naval presence.




## Commercial operators

Shipping firms, insurers, and crews transformed isolated attacks into a durable logistics story once they began rerouting at scale.


### Trade and insurance decisions




#### Commercial carriers

The operational choices of liner companies and bulk operators became the clearest indicator of whether the corridor still felt usable.

- Role: Traffic and routing baseline
- Geographic anchor: Concentrated in the southern Red Sea transit lane and the alternate Cape route once risk pricing shifted.
- Why it matters: Routing decisions are what turned a regional threat into a global trade disruption story.
- What to monitor: Transit volumes, insurer terms, crew confidence, and how long diversions remain economically acceptable.
